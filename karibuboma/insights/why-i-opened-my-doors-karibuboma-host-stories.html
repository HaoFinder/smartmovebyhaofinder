Why I Opened My Doors: Real Stories from KaribuBoma Hosts

July 24, 2026 8 min read By KaribuBoma Team

Behind every KaribuBoma stay is a host — someone who has chosen to open their home, share their space, and welcome a stranger into their life. These hosts come from all walks of life: teachers, artists, business owners, retirees, students. They are united by a common belief in the power of hospitality and the beauty of human connection.

In this collection, we share the stories of hosts from across Kenya. Their words speak to the heart of what KaribuBoma is about: trust, generosity, and the simple joy of welcoming someone into your home.

The host's heart:

Opening your home is an act of trust and generosity. These stories show that what you receive in return is far greater than what you give.

Grace Wanjiru — Kilimani, Nairobi

"I didn't join KaribuBoma because I needed money — I joined because I was curious. I had a spare room, and I thought, 'Why not invite the world in?' The first guest was a young woman from Germany who was traveling solo. I was nervous. Would she be respectful? Would she feel comfortable in my home? But from the moment she arrived, I felt like I had known her for years. We shared meals, we laughed, we walked through the neighborhood. She taught me about her culture, and I taught her about mine. When she left, she hugged me and said, 'Thank you for making me feel like family.' That was the moment I realized what KaribuBoma is really about. It's not about having a guest — it's about gaining a family member."

David Ochieng — Westlands, Nairobi

"I grew up in a community where hospitality was a way of life. My grandmother always said, 'A guest is a blessing.' When I heard about KaribuBoma, it felt like a natural extension of what I grew up with. My first guest was a Canadian journalist who was writing a story about Nairobi's tech scene. We stayed up late talking about everything — politics, music, the future of Kenya. By the end of his stay, we were friends. He's come back to visit twice since then. Hosting has reminded me of the beauty of connection. It's easy to get lost in our own lives, but when you open your home to a stranger, you're reminded of how much we all share."

Faith Akinyi — Kisumu

"I live by the lake, and I always thought it was a shame that so many travelers come to Kisumu and never really experience the lake — not just the view, but the life around it. So I decided to host. My first guest was a backpacker from Australia who was traveling through East Africa. I took him to Dunga Beach, introduced him to the fishermen, and we had a meal of fresh tilapia by the water. He told me it was the most authentic experience he'd had in Kenya. That made me so happy. I realized that hosting isn't just about sharing a room — it's about sharing your home, your community, and your way of life. Every guest who stays with me becomes part of my story."

James Mwangi — Kileleshwa, Nairobi

"I'm a teacher, and I've always believed that learning happens everywhere — not just in the classroom. When I heard about KaribuBoma, I thought it would be a way to bring the world to my home. And it has. I've hosted travelers from 15 countries, and every single one has taught me something. I've learned about different education systems, different parenting styles, different ways of seeing the world. And I've shared my own culture — I've taught guests how to cook ugali, how to greet elders, how to understand the rhythm of Nairobi life. Hosting has made me a better teacher and a more curious human being."

Amina Hassan — Mombasa

"I live in Mombasa, a city full of history and culture. I wanted to share that with people from other parts of the world. My first guest was a history student from the UK who was fascinated by Swahili culture. I took her to the Old Town, showed her the spice markets, and explained the history of Fort Jesus. She was so engaged and curious. We had deep conversations about colonialism, identity, and the future of the Swahili coast. After she left, she sent me a message: 'You opened my eyes to a world I never knew existed.' That's what hosting is about — opening eyes, opening minds, and opening hearts."

What Hosting Means to Them

Across all these stories, certain themes emerge: the joy of connection, the beauty of cultural exchange, and the deep satisfaction of welcoming someone into your home. Hosts consistently say that they receive far more than they give — in the form of friendship, perspective, and a broader understanding of the world.

"I signed up to share my home — but I've received so much more. I've received friends, stories, and a new appreciation for the diversity of human experience. Hosting has made my world bigger." — Peter Njoroge, Host in Nakuru

How You Can Join Them

If these stories inspire you, consider joining the KaribuBoma community as a host. You don't need a fancy home — a spare room, a comfortable couch, or even a guest wing can be enough. What matters is your willingness to welcome, share, and connect.

Here are a few steps to get started:

  • Complete your verification: Build trust with guests by completing Tier 1, 2, or 3 verification.
  • Create your listing: Share what you offer, your interests, and what makes your home special.
  • Welcome your first guest: Start with a short stay to get comfortable with the experience.
  • Enjoy the journey: Every guest brings a new story, a new perspective, and a new friendship.
